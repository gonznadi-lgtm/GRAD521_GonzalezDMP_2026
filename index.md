# Data description
For my research project, I am looking at the role of p38, a MAP kinase, in Parkinson’s disease (PD). I am looking at its effect on environmentally induced and familial forms of PD. My project focuses on three aims, the first of which is the impact of p38 overexpression and knockout in environmentally induced PD. This aim is to understand p38’s role in cellular processes, as previous work has shown that when p38 is knocked down, we observe PD-like symptoms, and when p38 is overexpressed, it’s protective against PD-like phenotypes. Interestingly, in the disease state, p38 is persistently activated, which in turn leads to disease progression. In my second aim, I am examining the activation state of p38 and its role in disease progression. P38 is an enzyme that undergoes dual phosphorylation. I am examining various Drosophila constructs in which p38 exhibits different phosphorylation states and performing experiments on those flies. My third aim is to look at p38’s role in protein autophagic regulation in familial forms, as p38 has been shown to interact with parkin, a commonly mutated gene in PD that is also involved in the autophagosome degradation pathway.
# Roles and responsibilities 
Effective data management in my research project requires clearly defined roles to ensure data integrity, reproducibility, and compliance with institutional and funder requirements. My project focuses on investigating the role of p38 signaling in pathways implicated in Parkinson’s disease using a Drosophila melanogaster model. This work generates multiple types of data, including qPCR results, Seahorse metabolic assay outputs, behavioral assay data, and confocal microscopy images of fly brains to assess neuronal health and mitochondrial dysfunction.

As the graduate student leading the experimental work, I am responsible for implementing the Data Management Plan. This includes organizing all datasets, maintaining consistent file naming conventions, and ensuring that all data is properly documented. I am responsible for data collection and generation, including performing qPCR experiments, conducting Seahorse assays to measure mitochondrial function, carrying out Drosophila behavioral assays (climbing or locomotion assays), and acquiring confocal microscopy images. I am also responsible for data organization, metadata generation, and quality control. For each dataset, I will record relevant metadata, including experimental conditions, genotypes, qPCR primer sequences, Seahorse assay parameters, and confocal imaging settings (laser intensity, magnification). I will perform quality control checks to verify raw data outputs, identify potential technical errors, and ensure that biological and technical replicates are accurately recorded. In addition, I will perform data analysis and software management, including processing qPCR data, analyzing Seahorse metabolic outputs, and quantifying imaging data (neuronal integrity and mitochondrial morphology). Any scripts or computational workflows used in analysis will be documented and version-controlled to ensure reproducibility.

My Principal Investigator provides oversight and guidance for all aspects of data management. The PI is responsible for ensuring that data management practices comply with institutional and funding agency requirements and that appropriate resources are available for secure storage, backup, and long-term preservation. The PI also contributes to data interpretation and ensures that data are appropriately prepared for publication and sharing. Although the project involves only the PI and me, responsibilities are clearly defined. If I am unable to continue the project, all data, metadata, and analysis workflows will be stored in shared, organized lab repositories. This will allow the PI or another researcher to access and continue the work with minimal disruption.

My project must comply with Oregon State University's institutional data policies, including guidelines for secure data storage, retention, and responsible data management. If funded by agencies such as the National Institutes of Health, I will also adhere to their data management and sharing requirements, including submitting a DMP and publicly sharing the data underlying publications. Additionally, journals in neuroscience and molecular biology typically require that data supporting published findings be made publicly available in appropriate repositories.

During the project, I will store data on secure, institutionally managed servers provided by Oregon State University, which offers controlled access and regular maintenance. Raw data generated from instruments (qPCR machines, Seahorse analyzers, and confocal microscopes) will be transferred promptly from instrument computers to centralized storage to reduce the risk of data loss. Working datasets may temporarily reside on my local computer but will be routinely synchronized with secure institutional storage. I may also use secure, institutionally supported cloud platforms for data organization and collaboration with my PI.

To ensure data security, I will implement a multi-layered backup strategy. I will maintain at least three copies of all critical data: a primary working copy, an automatic backup on institutional servers, and a secondary backup on a secure cloud storage platform. In addition, I will perform periodic manual backups of critical datasets, such as processed imaging data and final analysis outputs. Version control systems will be used to manage analysis scripts and track changes over time. If I use shared network drives, I will verify that they are included in institutional backup systems. To ensure long-term access to data after I graduate, I will transfer ownership of all cloud-based files and datasets to my PI. This will ensure that all data remains accessible and preserved beyond my direct involvement in the project.
